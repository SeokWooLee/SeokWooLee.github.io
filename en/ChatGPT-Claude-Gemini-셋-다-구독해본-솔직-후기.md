# ChatGPT vs Claude vs Gemini: What's Actually Different? (Updated July 2026)

These days it feels like everyone's using some kind of AI. But when you actually try to pick one, it gets confusing fast.

Should you go with ChatGPT? People say Claude is better. And apparently Gemini's free tier is pretty generous, too... I ended up paying for all three just to figure it out for myself, and it took a while.

To cut to the chase: as of July 2026, ChatGPT has finally reached a point where it's genuinely solid for just about anyone, developers in the trenches still lean toward Claude for real work, and Gemini, honestly, is still a step behind.

And this month happens to be a big one for changes. OpenAI dropped GPT-5.6 on July 9th, and Anthropic's top-tier model, Claude Fable 5, went fully open to everyone starting July 1st. Meanwhile, Google's Gemini 3.5 Pro has been pushed back to July 17th.

## A Quick Side-by-Side

All three have free tiers, and their standard paid plans hover around $20/month. But once you look at the full lineup of plans, the differences are bigger than you'd expect.

| | ChatGPT | Claude | Gemini |
|---|---|---|---|
| Maker | OpenAI | Anthropic | Google |
| Latest model | GPT-5.6 (Sol · Terra · Luna) | Claude Fable 5 | Gemini 3.1 Pro |
| Release | July 9, 2026 | June 9, 2026 (fully open July 1) | 3.5 Pro delayed to July 17 |
| Entry plan | Go, $8/mo | None | AI Plus, $7.99/mo |
| Standard plan | Plus, $20/mo | Pro, $20/mo (~$17/mo with annual billing) | AI Pro, $19.99/mo |
| Top-tier plan | Pro, $100–200/mo | Max, $100–200/mo | AI Ultra, $99.99–200/mo |
| Strengths | Versatility, speed and value | Coding, writing, reliability | Search/Google integration, generous free tier |

Looking at just the standard plans, all three land around $20 and seem similar — but the tiers above and below that tell a different story. ChatGPT and Gemini both offer an $8/month entry tier for people who want to start light, while Claude's paid plans start at $20. On the flip side, the premium plans for heavy users have all converged around $100–200/month across the board.

> In 2026, the right way to pick an AI isn't by ranking raw performance — it's by matching the tool to what you actually do.

## ChatGPT — No Longer Just "Good Enough," Now a True All-Rounder

ChatGPT is still the AI the most people use. And starting with GPT-5.6 on July 9th, it's finally earned the right to shed the "jack of all trades, master of none" label.

GPT-5.6 comes in three tiers ranked by capability — Sol, Terra, and Luna. The flagship, Sol, delivers top-tier results in coding, knowledge work, and science while using fewer tokens and costing less. Its API pricing is half that of Claude Fable 5.

Image generation, voice conversations, data analysis, and custom GPTs are all still available in one app. I usually recommend ChatGPT to people who are just getting started with AI — there's an overwhelming amount of material out there, so when you get stuck, a quick search almost always turns up the answer.

- Best for: AI beginners, anyone who wants a wide range of features in one place, value-conscious users
- Downside: There's some controversy over whether real-world reliability matches its benchmark scores (the evaluation group METR flagged GPT-5.6 Sol for having the highest "reward hacking" rate among publicly available models)

## Claude — The AI Developers Actually Choose for Real Work

Claude is Anthropic's AI, best known for handling long documents and for its coding chops.

The newly released Claude Fable 5 is the highest tier Anthropic has ever made publicly available. There was a brief scare right after its June 9th launch, when U.S. export controls temporarily restricted access, but it reopened worldwide on July 1st.

Using it firsthand, the writing quality is noticeably more natural. I found it especially satisfying for tasks where the quality of the prose really matters — draft reports, blog posts, emails.

And as for coding — I'll get into that in more detail below — but among developers, it's essentially become the default choice.

- Best for: developers, professionals who write a lot, anyone who regularly works with long documents
- Downside: the priciest of the three on an API basis, and its feature set is leaner compared to ChatGPT's

---

## Gemini — Google's Ecosystem Is Powerful, But Still a Step Behind

Gemini's biggest asset is Google itself. It's woven into services you're probably already using — Search, Gmail, Google Docs, YouTube. And its free tier remaining generous is still a real advantage.

But to be honest, as of July 2026, it's a step behind in the model race.

While its rivals were rolling out GPT-5.6 and Claude Fable 5, Google's next release, Gemini 3.5 Pro, got pushed back to July 17th while its architecture gets overhauled. The best model currently available is Gemini 3.1 Pro, and it lags behind the other two on coding benchmarks (on SWE-bench Pro, Gemini 3.1 Pro scores 54.2% versus Claude Opus 4.8's 69.2%).

Of course, its unique strengths — deep integration with Google's services, long context handling, and a generous free tier — still hold. And once 3.5 Pro ships, the landscape could shift again.

- Best for: heavy users of Google's services, anyone who wants to start for free
- Downside: currently trailing in the latest model race, with a particularly wide gap in coding performance

## How Do They Actually Perform on Coding? — Benchmarks vs. Developer Sentiment

Coding is easily the hottest battleground in AI right now, so it deserves its own section.

**The benchmarks are close.** On SWE-bench Pro, which measures the ability to resolve real GitHub issues, Claude Fable 5 tops the published rankings at 80.3% (OpenAI hasn't disclosed Sol's score). But on Terminal-Bench 2.1, which measures command-line proficiency, GPT-5.6 Sol edges ahead at 88.8% versus Claude Fable 5's 83.4%.

**But developer sentiment leans toward Claude.** In one developer community survey, OpenAI Codex led on raw vote count at 65.3%. But when responses were weighted by how many upvotes they received, preference flipped toward Claude at 79.9%. In other words: Codex gets more votes simply because it's used daily, but when it comes to the more substantive question of "which one is actually better," Claude wins by a wide margin.

In practice, Claude Code is also considered the coding agent with the most enterprise adoption. There's even a saying going around — "type with Codex, commit with Claude Code" — reflecting how common it is to use OpenAI's tools for fast autocomplete, while handing off the serious work of finishing code and pushing it to a repository to Claude.

Gemini CLI's generous free usage makes it a good entry point, but as the numbers above show, its actual coding performance still hasn't caught up to the other two.

To sum it up:

- Coding benchmarks: Claude and ChatGPT split the top spot depending on the category; Gemini lags behind
- Developer preference: Claude wins among working developers and enterprise adoption
- Value: ChatGPT's API pricing is half the cost, giving it an edge for high-volume use

## So Which One Should You Pick?

After using all three myself, here's where I landed:

- Just starting out: ChatGPT (tons of resources, wide feature set, and now top-tier performance too)
- Developer or writing is your main job: Claude (developers' top pick, strongest prose)
- Heavy Google user or want to stay on the free tier: Gemini (great integration and free perks, though performance trails a bit)

Let me wrap up with a quick Q&A on some common questions.

**Q. Is the free tier enough on its own?**
If you just want to ask questions casually and get answers, the free tier on any of the three is plenty. If free feels limiting but $20 feels like too much, consider stepping up to an $8/month entry tier like ChatGPT Go or Gemini AI Plus first.

**Q. Do I need to subscribe to more than one?**
Most people are fine with just one. That said, a lot of developers actually run two — using Claude for coding and ChatGPT for everything else.

**Q. Does the performance ranking change often?**
Yes, it flips every few months. This month alone, GPT-5.6 just launched and Gemini 3.5 Pro is still on the way. So rather than chasing rankings, picking what fits your actual use case tends to hold up better over time.

## Wrapping Up

There's no single right answer when it comes to choosing an AI — but there are wrong ones. Namely, picking whatever everyone else says is best without checking whether it actually fits what you do.

Start by asking all three the same question on their free tiers. Give it a week, and you'll naturally see which one clicks with you. 😊
